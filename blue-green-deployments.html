Blue-Green Deployments on EKS

How we cut monthly maintenance downtime by 85% without touching our release cadence.

Every month, our team would schedule a 2-hour maintenance window to deploy updates to 20+ Pega microservices running on Amazon EKS. Users got an email. We got anxiety. And more often than not, something would go sideways during the cutover and we'd be scrambling at midnight.

After one particularly rough deployment, I proposed we implement a proper Blue-Green deployment strategy across availability zones. Six weeks later, our maintenance windows went from 2 hours to under 18 minutes — and most of that is just health check wait time.

85% downtime reduction
20+ services migrated
0 rollback failures

What is Blue-Green anyway?

The idea is simple: you maintain two identical environments — Blue (live) and Green (idle). When you deploy, you push to Green, run your smoke tests, and then shift traffic. If something breaks, you shift traffic back to Blue. No scrambling. No midnight rollbacks from a broken state.

On Kubernetes, this translates to running two sets of Deployments simultaneously and controlling traffic via a Service selector switch. On EKS specifically, we layer in ALB Ingress weighted target groups to make the traffic shift gradual and controllable.

The Setup

Two deployments, one service

Each microservice gets two Deployment manifests — one labeled slot: blue, one labeled slot: green. The Service selector points to whichever slot is currently live. 

deployment-blue.yaml
apiVersion: apps/v1
kind: Deployment
metadata:
  name: pega-web-blue
spec:
  replicas: 3
  selector:
    matchLabels:
      app: pega-web
      slot: blue
  template:
    metadata:
      labels:
        app: pega-web
        slot: blue
    spec:
      containers:
        - name: pega-web
          image: quay.io/carelon/pega-web:v8.8
          ports:
            - containerPort: 8080
service.yaml
apiVersion: v1
kind: Service
metadata:
  name: pega-web
spec:
  selector:
    app: pega-web
    slot: blue   # ← this is the only line that changes on cutover
  ports:
    - port: 80
      targetPort: 8080

The cutover script

The actual cutover is a single kubectl patch command. We wrapped this in a Jenkins pipeline step with a manual approval gate before execution. 

cutover.sh
#!/bin/bash
CURRENT=$(kubectl get svc pega-web \
  -o jsonpath='{.spec.selector.slot}')

if [ "$CURRENT" = "blue" ]; then
  NEW_SLOT="green"
else
  NEW_SLOT="blue"
fi

echo "Switching traffic from $CURRENT → $NEW_SLOT"

kubectl patch svc pega-web \
  -p "{\"spec\":{\"selector\":{\"slot\":\"$NEW_SLOT\"}}}"

echo "Done. Verify at: https://pega.internal"
💡 Always keep the idle slot running at reduced replica count (1 pod) between deployments. Scaling it up fresh right before a deployment adds unnecessary time to your window.

Across Availability Zones

The trickier part was making this work across AZs. We use EKS managed node groups spread across ap-south-1a, ap-south-1b, and ap-south-1c. Pod affinity rules ensure Blue pods and Green pods land on different AZs, so a single AZ failure doesn't take down both slots simultaneously. 

pod affinity
affinity:
  podAntiAffinity:
    preferredDuringSchedulingIgnoredDuringExecution:
      - weight: 100
        podAffinityTerm:
          labelSelector:
            matchExpressions:
              - key: slot
                operator: In
                values: ["blue", "green"]
          topologyKey: topology.kubernetes.io/zone

What we got wrong first

  • Session stickiness. Pega is stateful. We forgot to configure ALB sticky sessions on the target group, so users mid-session got dropped during the first cutover test. Fixed with stickiness.enabled: true on the TargetGroupBinding.
  • Health check timing. Our initial health check grace period was too short. Green pods would pass the readiness probe but Pega's internal startup took another 40 seconds. Added a startupProbe with a longer failureThreshold.
  • ConfigMap drift. Blue and Green were pulling from the same ConfigMap. When we updated config for a Green deploy, it immediately affected Blue too. We now maintain slot-specific ConfigMaps and merge them at deploy time via Ansible.

Results

After rolling this out across all 5 environments (dev, sit, uat, perf, prod), our monthly deployment story changed completely. What used to be a stress-filled 2-hour window that sometimes stretched to 4 is now a calm 18-minute operation with a clear rollback path at every stage.

The team stopped dreading deployment Fridays. That alone made it worth the six weeks of work.
